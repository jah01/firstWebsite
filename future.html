Data

     Big data is a term that describes the large volume of data – both structured and unstructured – that inundates a business on a day-to-day basis. But it’s not the amount of data that’s important. It’s what organizations do with the data that matters. Big data can be analyzed for insights that lead to better decisions and strategic business moves.
     The importance of big data doesn’t revolve around how much data you have, but what you do with it. You can take data from any source and analyze it to find answers that enable 1) cost reductions, 2) time reductions, 3) new product development and optimized offerings, and 4) smart decision making.

Who Uses Big Data?

Banking

     With large amounts of information streaming in from countless sources, banks are faced with finding new and innovative ways to manage big data. While it’s important to understand customers and boost their satisfaction, it’s equally important to minimize risk and fraud while maintaining regulatory compliance. Big data brings big insights, but it also requires financial institutions to stay one step ahead of the game with advanced analytics.

Education

     Educators armed with data-driven insight can make a significant impact on school systems, students and curriculums. By analyzing big data, they can identify at-risk students, make sure students are making adequate progress, and can implement a better system for evaluation and support of teachers and principals.

Government

     When government agencies are able to harness and apply analytics to their big data, they gain significant ground when it comes to managing utilities, running agencies, dealing with traffic congestion or preventing crime. But while there are many advantages to big data, governments must also address issues of transparency and privacy.

Health Care

     Patient records. Treatment plans. Prescription information. When it comes to health care, everything needs to be done quickly, accurately – and, in some cases, with enough transparency to satisfy stringent industry regulations. When big data is managed effectively, health care providers can uncover hidden insights that improve patient care.

Manufacturing

     Armed with insight that big data can provide, manufacturers can boost quality and output while minimizing waste – processes that are key in today’s highly competitive market. More and more manufacturers are working in an analytics-based culture, which means they can solve problems faster and make more agile business decisions.

Retail

     Customer relationship building is critical to the retail industry – and the best way to manage that is to manage big data. Retailers need to know the best way to market to customers, the most effective way to handle transactions, and the most strategic way to bring back lapsed business. Big data remains at the heart of all those things.

     To find out where computers and AI will lead everyday life in the future, Pew Research Center conducted a study to see the opinions of experts:
     The essential question introduced by Pew Research was “In the next 10 years, do you think we will see the emergence of new educational and training programs that can successfully train large numbers of workers in the skills they will need to perform the jobs of the future?” (2). Pew explained there were a variety of responses, many arguing a new training system must be found. Another popular stance was that humans must retain their unique skills of problem solving, emotion, and interaction. Simon Gottschalk, a professor at the University of Nevada stated “‘the skills necessary at higher echelons will… display intercultural sensitivity, marketing, and… “social” and “emotional” intelligence’” that separates humans from AI.
     After receiving personal opinions from over one thousand individuals, Pew Research broke down the responses into five themes about the future of jobs training: three hopeful and two concerning.
     The first theme stated: “The training ecosystem will evolve, with a mix of education formats” (5). Michael Wollowski supports this theme because he is a “‘big believer in the college experience’” that builds “‘in [one’s] own self and [one’s] abilities’” (6). Justin Write of MIT brings up “‘educators have always found new ways of training the next generation of students for the jobs of the future, and this generation will be no different’” (6) Conversely, Jerry Michalski argues institutions “‘take too long to teach impractical skills and knowledge not connected to the real world,’” meaning “‘they mostly fail’” at their task of educating the next generation (7). Still, many say the traditional college is not needed when there is augmented reality (AR), virtual reality (VR), and AI. AR and VR are great for seeing things in the outside world such as looking at Mount Everest from the comfort of one’s home, or maybe an animation showing what is below Earth’s crust. These tools are “cost-effective, evolving for the better, and game-changing because they are globally accessible,” so studying online may become more popular than a classroom (7). Adding on, Tawny Schlieski explains they “‘make learning experimental,… engaging students with physical movement, and they enable interactive and responsive instructional assets’” (8). Although the engaging experience may be a positive attribute to independent learning, Uta Russman, a professor at Vienna, objects by saying in the future, “‘more jobs will require highly sophisticated people whose skills cannot be trained in “mass” online programs’” (9).
     Theme Two was a little more straight-forward, saying “learners must cultivate 21st-century skills” (9). “Tough to teach skills… ‘requiring emotional intelligence, empathy, compassion, and creative judgement and discernment’” will no doubt emerge as valuable traits in the future, Suzanne Price of Continuum Analytics explains (9-10). An anonymous instructional designer added “‘the key to the future will be flexibility and personal motivation to learn and tinker with new things’” (11).
     The next theme predicts “New credential systems will arise” as independent learning becomes more popular (11). Professor William J. Ward supports online education as opposed to traditional education because online is “‘more transparent and [does] a better job on delivering skills’” (12).
     Theme Four and Theme Five insist the jobs market will not be able to hold up and that capitalism will not be able to function, respectively. “Many of [the respondents] say that current K-12… programs are incapable of making adjustments within the next decade” to catch up to the jobs market (13). Thomas Claburn, an editor at Information Week, claimed he is “‘skeptical that education programs can keep pace with technology’” (14). The decline of jobs is also something many respondents looked at. “‘The problem of future jobs is not one of skills training -- it is one of diminishing jobs,’” entrepreneur Jennifer Zickerman says. Without any jobs at all, why would skills training be an issue? It is clear Ms. Zickerman would rather focus on ensuring jobs first and worrying about training later. The rest of the article explores more individual responses such as the support of institutions or independent work.
